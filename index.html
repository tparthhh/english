Job Application Letter

A formal letter applying for a job advertised in a newspaper or otherwise. Tests your ability to present yourself professionally in writing.

⭐ 5 Marks
📝 ~150 words
⏱ 10–12 min
Format
Bio-Data ★
Full Examples
Scoring
Tips & Traps
Perfect Format — Step by Step
Job Application Letter Template
Sender's AddressYour full address (3–4 lines)
DateDay Month Year (e.g., 10 March 2026)
Receiver's AddressThe Hiring Manager / HR Manager
Name of Company / Organization
City — Pincode
SubjectApplication for the post of [Job Title]
SalutationSir / Madam,

Para 1 (Opening)Source of info + post applied for + desire to apply
Para 2 (Credentials)Qualifications + marks/grade + skills + experience
Para 3 (Closing)Express eagerness + request interview + reference to CV

Complimentary CloseYours faithfully,
Signature[Your Name]
Key Rules — Never Break These

✓ Always Do

  • Mention the exact source (e.g., "The Hindu, 10 March 2026")
  • Use "Yours faithfully" (not sincerely, for unknown person)
  • Mention enclosure of Bio-Data / CV
  • Write in formal, impersonal language
  • Keep each paragraph focused on one idea

✗ Never Do

  • Don't use "I" in the Subject line
  • Don't write in casual/informal language
  • Don't skip the Sender's or Receiver's address
  • Don't use "Yours sincerely" for strangers
  • Don't forget to mention the post name in Subject
What is a Bio-Data?

In CBSE Class 12, after writing the application letter, you must write the Bio-Data as a separate enclosure in the same answer. It is a structured personal profile of the applicant. It is written below the letter, clearly marked, and is worth marks in the content section.

⚠️ CBSE Rule: The Bio-Data must be written as part of your answer. Writing only "Enc: Bio-Data" without actually writing it can cost you 1–2 marks in content.

Key Rules for Bio-Data in CBSE
  • Write "BIO-DATA" as a heading, centred, after the letter — never skip it.
  • The Educational Qualifications table must always be present — it's a key content point.
  • If you are a Class XII student applying, write "2026 — Appearing" for your current exam.
  • The Declaration line is mandatory — examiners look for it.
  • End with Place, Date, and your Signature + Name.
  • Keep it to one page equivalent — don't over-pad, don't under-write.
💡 In the CBSE exam, the Bio-Data is written immediately after the letter as the enclosure. Both together form one complete answer.

How CBSE Awards Marks
ParameterWhat Examiners Look ForMarks

Full 5-Mark Strategy
  • Write all format elements — examiners tick each one.
  • Mention source: newspaper + date = instant credibility point.
  • Para 2 should have at least 2 specific credentials (subject, score, skill).
  • Use varied formal phrases: "I wish to apply", "I beg to state", "I am confident".
  • End with "Enc: Bio-Data" — this is a separate mark trigger.
  • No spelling mistakes in format parts (address, subject, salutation).
Killer Opening Lines (Ready to Use)
1. "I am writing in response to the advertisement published in [newspaper] dated [date]…"
2. "With reference to your advertisement in [newspaper], I wish to apply for the post of…"
3. "I beg to apply for the post of ___ advertised in [newspaper] on [date]…"
Power Phrases for Para 2
• "I have scored ___% in my CBSE Class XII Board Examinations…"
• "I am proficient in ___ and have hands-on experience in ___…"
• "During my time at ___, I developed strong skills in ___…"
• "I hold a degree/certificate in ___ from ___ University…"
Closing Line Bank
• "I shall be grateful if you consider my candidature and call me for an interview."
• "I look forward to the opportunity of a personal interview at your convenience."
• "Enclosed herewith is my Bio-Data for your kind reference and consideration."

Article Writing

Articles are written for newspapers, magazines, or school journals on a given topic. Tests your ability to express, inform, and engage readers.

⭐ 6 Marks
📝 150–200 words
⏱ 15 min
Format
Examples
Tips
Article Format
Article Structure
Title / HeadingBold, catchy, relevant — centered
By-lineBy [Your Name / Given Name]

Paragraph 1 (Hook)Start with a question, fact, or quote — introduce the topic
Para 2 (Body 1)Main point 1 — causes, background, statistics
Para 3 (Body 2)Main point 2 — effects, opinions, examples
Para 4 (Conclusion)Solutions, call to action, forward-looking statement
Example 1
Social Media: A Boon or a Bane?
By Arjun Mehta
Are we truly connecting with the world, or merely scrolling through an illusion? Social media, which began as a tool for communication, has now become the lens through which millions perceive reality. With over 5 billion active users globally, its influence is undeniable — but so are its consequences.
On the one hand, social media has democratised information, empowered activists, and bridged geographical distances. Businesses have found new markets, artists have found audiences, and ordinary citizens have found their voices. It has been a lifeline especially during the COVID-19 pandemic when physical connectivity was severed.
On the other hand, cyberbullying, misinformation, and digital addiction pose serious threats — especially to teenagers. Studies indicate that excessive social media use correlates with anxiety and poor self-esteem among adolescents. The algorithm, designed to maximise engagement, often amplifies outrage over reason.
The solution lies not in abandoning these platforms, but in using them mindfully. Digital literacy must be integrated into school curricula. Parents and educators must guide young users to treat social media as a tool — not a lifestyle. Used wisely, it remains one of the most powerful forces for positive change in modern society.
Example 2
The Silent Crisis: Mental Health Among Students
By Rhea Kapoor
"Education is not the learning of facts, but the training of the mind to think," said Einstein. Yet today, millions of students struggle not with academic concepts but with anxiety, depression, and burnout — a crisis hidden behind the façade of competitive examinations and mounting expectations.
According to a WHO report, one in seven Indian adolescents aged 13–17 years experiences a mental health disorder. Academic pressure, fear of failure, parental expectations, and social isolation are among the leading triggers. Board examinations, in particular, are notorious for causing extreme stress — sometimes with tragic consequences.
The problem is further compounded by stigma. In many Indian households, mental health is still considered a sign of weakness. Students suffer in silence, unwilling to seek help for fear of judgment. Schools often lack trained counsellors, and parents rarely recognise early warning signs.
It is time we reimagined education as a holistic experience — one that nurtures emotional intelligence alongside academic ability. Schools must appoint qualified counsellors, parents must foster open conversations, and students must be taught that seeking help is an act of strength, not weakness. Our children's minds deserve as much care as their report cards.
🔥
Start with a HookUse a rhetorical question, shocking statistic, or powerful quote in the very first line.
🎨
Choose a Catchy TitleAvoid "Article on Pollution". Instead: "Breath at Risk: India's Battle Against Pollution".
📐
4 Paragraphs RuleIntro → Body 1 → Body 2 → Conclusion. Never merge or skip.
💬
Use Discourse Markers"Furthermore", "On the other hand", "Consequently", "In conclusion" — show logical flow.
📊
Add a StatisticOne well-placed fact/statistic gives your article authority and earns expression marks.
✍️
Active VoiceWrite mostly in active voice. "The government must act" not "Action must be taken by the government".

Do's & Don'ts

Always Include

  • Name of school / organisation at top
  • The word NOTICE in caps and centred
  • Date on left side
  • Clear heading/title
  • All 5 Ws in the body
  • Name + Designation at bottom

Never Do

  • Don't exceed 50–70 words
  • Don't write in first person ("I am pleased...")
  • Don't add unnecessary details
  • Don't miss the date
  • Don't forget to draw a box around the notice
🎙️ Writing Section

Speech Writing

A speech is a formal piece of writing meant to be delivered orally. It must engage the audience, present a clear argument, and end memorably.

⭐ 6 Marks
📝 150–200 words
⏱ 15 min
Speech Format
Speech Structure
TitleSpeech on [Topic]
AddressGood morning / Good afternoon, Respected Principal, Teachers, and Dear Friends,
IntroductionState your name + topic. Hook the audience.
Body2–3 paragraphs with main points, evidence, examples
ConclusionCall to action / memorable closing line
Thank YouThank you for your patient hearing.
Example
Speech on Importance of Environmental Conservation
Good morning, Respected Principal, Teachers, and Dear Fellow Students,
I, Nisha Rao of Class XII B, stand before you today to speak on a topic that is no longer merely academic — it is a matter of survival: Environmental Conservation.
Our planet is warming at an unprecedented rate. Glaciers are melting, sea levels are rising, and species are disappearing faster than scientists can document them. The IPCC has warned that we have less than a decade to limit global warming to 1.5°C — or face irreversible consequences. And yet, we continue deforesting, polluting, and consuming as if the Earth is infinite.
But there is hope. Renewable energy adoption is accelerating. Communities around the world are planting billions of trees. Individuals — including young people like us — are demanding change. Even small acts, like reducing plastic use, conserving water, or choosing public transport, collectively create massive impact.
Friends, we did not inherit this Earth from our ancestors — we are borrowing it from our children. Let us choose wisely what we leave behind.
Thank you for your patient hearing.

Debate Writing

A debate is a structured argument for or against a given motion. You will be asked to write either FOR or AGAINST a given statement.

⭐ 6 Marks
📝 150–200 words
⏱ 15 min
Debate Format
Debate Structure
Title"Motion: [Statement]" — For / Against
AddressRespected Chairperson, Honourable Judges, and Dear Audience,
OpeningState which side you represent + motion clearly
Arguments3 strong points with evidence/examples for your side
CounterBriefly acknowledge opponent's view, then refute it
ConclusionRestate your position with a memorable one-liner
CloseI rest my case. / Thank you.
Useful Debate Phrases
Opening: "I stand before you to firmly support / oppose the motion that…"
Adding Points: "Furthermore… / What is more… / To add to this…"
Refuting: "My opponents claim… however, the facts suggest otherwise…"
Citing evidence: "According to… / Research indicates… / Statistics reveal…"
Concluding: "In light of these compelling arguments, I urge this house to…"
📋 Writing Section

Report Writing

A report is a factual, objective account of an event that has already occurred — written for a newspaper or an authority figure.

⭐ 6 Marks
📝 150–200 words
⏱ 15 min
Report Format
Newspaper Report / School Report
HeadlineBold, catchy, in Title Case
By-lineBy [Name], Staff Reporter / School Correspondent
Place & DateDelhi, 10 March: …
Lead ParaWho, What, When, Where — answer all in first paragraph
BodyDetails, quotes, background information
ConclusionImpact, future plans, official statement
Key Rules for Reports
  • Always use past tense (the event has already happened).
  • Write in third person — never "I" or "we".
  • Use reported speech for quotes: "The Principal stated that…"
  • Be factual and objective — no personal opinions.
  • Start with the most important information (inverted pyramid style).

📖 Reading Section A

Reading Passage I — Factual

A factual/descriptive passage. Questions include Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs), True/False, and Short Answer questions. Tests comprehension and vocabulary.

⭐ 12 Marks
📝 600–900 words
⏱ 20 min
Reading Strategy — Full Marks Approach
  • Skim first (90 sec): Read headings and first sentence of each paragraph to understand the gist.
  • Read the questions next: Know what you're looking for before reading in detail.
  • Underline keywords in the passage as you read carefully.
  • For MCQs: Eliminate wrong options; find evidence in the passage for the right one.
  • For vocabulary: Use context clues — read the sentence before and after the word.
  • Write complete sentences for short answers — one-word answers lose marks.
Sample Passage — Practice Now

1In the dense rainforests of the Amazon basin, a silent revolution is underway. Scientists and conservationists are deploying cutting-edge technology — from drones to AI-powered sound analysis — to monitor biodiversity and detect illegal deforestation in real time. What was once an overwhelming, almost impossible task is gradually becoming manageable, offering a rare glimmer of hope for one of Earth's most critical ecosystems.

2The Amazon rainforest, often called the "lungs of the Earth", produces approximately 20% of the world's oxygen and is home to around 10% of all species on the planet. Yet in recent decades, it has faced relentless pressure from logging, agriculture, and mining. Between 2000 and 2020, Brazil lost over 400,000 square kilometres of forest — an area larger than the entire country of Germany. The consequences extend far beyond Brazil's borders, affecting global rainfall patterns, carbon cycles, and climate stability.

3Enter the era of "conservation technology." Organisations like the Rainforest Connection (RFCx) have deployed solar-powered listening devices in forest canopies. These devices record ambient sounds and transmit them to AI systems trained to recognise the specific acoustic signature of a chainsaw — a sound noticeably different from natural forest noise. When detected, real-time alerts are sent to forest rangers, enabling rapid response before significant damage occurs. The system has been deployed across nine countries, and its developers claim it can detect a chainsaw up to a kilometre away.

4Drones are another weapon in the conservationist's arsenal. Equipped with high-resolution cameras and thermal sensors, they can survey vast stretches of forest in hours — work that would take ground teams weeks. In some programmes, drones are even used to fire seed pods into deforested areas, accelerating reforestation at scale. Meanwhile, satellite imagery processed through machine learning algorithms can now detect changes in tree cover with remarkable precision, flagging newly cleared areas within 24 hours.

5Yet technology alone cannot save the rainforest. Experts caution that without robust policy frameworks, community involvement, and sustained funding, even the most sophisticated tools remain ineffective. Indigenous communities, who have protected these forests for centuries, must be recognised as equal partners in conservation — not afterthoughts. Their traditional ecological knowledge, combined with modern technology, represents the most powerful conservation toolkit available.

Questions — Based on Passage Above
1
What is the primary purpose of the AI-powered devices deployed by Rainforest Connection in the Amazon? (1 mark)
⌄
✓ Model Answer
The AI-powered devices are deployed to detect the sound of chainsaws — used in illegal logging — and send real-time alerts to forest rangers so they can respond rapidly before significant deforestation occurs.
2
According to the passage, how much forest did Brazil lose between 2000 and 2020? What does the author compare this area to? (2 marks)
⌄
✓ Model Answer
Brazil lost over 400,000 square kilometres of forest between 2000 and 2020. The author compares this area to the total land area of the country of Germany, using the comparison to emphasise the enormous scale of deforestation.
3
Find a word from paragraph 3 that means "the quality of a sound that distinguishes it from others." (1 mark)
⌄
✓ Model Answer
Acoustic signature (Para 3: "the specific acoustic signature of a chainsaw")
4
The author suggests that technology alone is sufficient to solve the deforestation crisis. True or False? Justify your answer with evidence from the passage. (2 marks)
⌄
✓ Model Answer
False. The author clearly states in paragraph 5 that "technology alone cannot save the rainforest." The passage further emphasises that "robust policy frameworks, community involvement, and sustained funding" are also needed, and that Indigenous communities must be included as equal partners in conservation efforts.
5
List any TWO ways in which drones are used for conservation in the Amazon, as described in the passage. (2 marks)
⌄
✓ Model Answer
(i) Drones equipped with high-resolution cameras and thermal sensors are used to survey vast stretches of forest quickly.
(ii) Drones are used to fire seed pods into deforested areas to accelerate reforestation at scale.
📝 Reading Section A

Reading Passage II — Discursive / Literary

A discursive or argumentative passage. Questions typically include note-making and summary writing, worth 8 marks total.

⭐ 10 Marks
📝 500–700 words
⏱ 18 min
Note-Making Format — Never Miss This
Note-Making Structure (5 marks)
TitleShort, relevant, centred — (½ mark)

1. Main Heading(based on para 1-2)
1.1 Sub-point
1.2 Sub-point

2. Main Heading(based on para 3-4)
2.1 Sub-point
2.2 Sub-point

Abbreviations KeyList 4–5 abbreviations used in notes — (1 mark)

Summary3rd person, 80 words, based ONLY on your notes — (3 marks)
Note-Making Rules
  • Use abbreviations throughout — list them separately. E.g., govt = government, env = environment.
  • Write in phrases, not full sentences in the notes section.
  • Use proper numbering: 1, 1.1, 1.2, 2, 2.1 — examiners check hierarchy.
  • The summary must be in third person and derived only from your notes.
  • Summary: exactly 80 words — aim for 75–85. Count carefully.
  • Do NOT lift sentences from the passage into summary — paraphrase.
